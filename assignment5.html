Box Tales

STEP 1

This is the story all about how I started creating a box that you can turn upside down. I started in Illustrator because I am beyond frustrated with solid work tools. I was able to visualize the pieces more clearly and how they would fit together. I combined that with some sketching to flesh out the idea. Similar to my press fit kit, I used out cropping pieces to hold the shape together.

STEP 2

In the next step, I exported a DXF from Illustrator to OnShape. In OnShape I extruded the paths and added my dogbones for milling.

STEP 3

Then I needed to set up some DXFs for milling. I exported the individual pieces then imported those pieces in KiCad to create the boards that I'd be milling with Bantam. In Bantam I adjusted the size of my material, the placement on the z-axis, and the starting x & y of the drill so that it wouldn't cut too close to the edge.

STEP 4

Milling! I changed tools a lot. I used a 1/8, which is the size I designed for, but didn't work out. Also a 1/16 that ended up actually doing all of my cutting, and a 1/32 that I decided not to cut with because it was too small. The first run through my pieces came out too big and wouldn't fit together.

Thanks to Dhwani, Hannah, Leo, and Josh for their help in this process.

STEP 5

Back to the drawing board. So I went back and redid my process from scratch and added a bit of math to find the perfect fit for my pieces. You can see in the sketch that I measured the fit to be exactly what it should be instead of just assuming the equal parts would fit.

STEP 6

RE-Milling. In my second milling attemp, my dogbones were seperate from the rest of the outline because of the wrong tool size. The last pieces I printed the the connecting male pieces were too small for the female parts, but the piece that printed wrong did fit. In the end I have a box that fits together, but couldn't pass a shake test if my life depended on it. I've identified the dogbones being the issue because my second piece that that did not cut the dogbones correctly did fit in the other pieces.
